What I learned by recreating a KPI review for an imaginary leadership team

Recreating a KPI review taught me that good analytics is not only about measuring performance. It is also about framing meaning, risk, and next steps for the people making decisions.

One useful exercise I keep coming back to is building analytics as if it were meant for a leadership meeting. Even if the company is imaginary, the exercise forces a more disciplined style of thinking. I have to decide which metrics matter, what trends deserve attention, and which explanations are worth saying out loud.

What stood out to me most is how important assumptions are. Metrics do not speak for themselves. A decline in conversion could point to messaging, seasonality, traffic quality, or process friction. Without stating what I believe is happening and why, the review remains descriptive rather than analytical.

Recommendations matter just as much. A leadership review should not stop at showing variance. It should guide the next conversation. That might mean suggesting a segment to investigate, a campaign to pause, or a process to monitor more closely. When recommendations are absent, dashboards and KPI packs can feel passive.

I also learned that restraint is valuable. Leaders usually need fewer charts and stronger interpretation. A clean review does not try to prove how much analysis was done. It tries to make the next decision easier.

That is the kind of analyst I want to become: someone who can move beyond reporting numbers and help shape what those numbers mean for the business.

Key Idea

Analysis should lead somewhere.

Good KPI reviews connect measurement with interpretation, tradeoffs, and next-step decisions.
