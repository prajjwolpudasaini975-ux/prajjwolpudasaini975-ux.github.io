My Journey So Far: From Reconciling Books to Reading Patterns

I didn't set out to leave finance. I set out to go deeper into it, and the data kept pulling me further in.

My journey began with a Bachelor of Business Administration at Kathmandu University, where Finance became the area I gravitated toward most. The mix of structure, logic, and real-world stakes appealed to me, and by the time I graduated, I knew I wanted to spend the early part of my career inside that world.

That path led me to Nabil Investment Banking Limited, where I joined as a Portfolio Intern and later moved into a Mutual Fund Operations Associate role. For nearly four years, I managed accounting for mutual fund schemes worth around £14.7 million in AUM, prepared quarterly regulatory reports, produced annual financial statements, and worked closely with clients across the fund's lifecycle. The currency of that world wasn't money so much as Excel: every reconciliation, every report, every client query traced back to a spreadsheet.

So I learned Excel, and somewhere along the way, it stopped being a tool and started becoming a language.

Somewhere between preparing my latest regulatory report and rebuilding a workflow that used to take days, something shifted. When I introduced automated reporting solutions that cut report preparation time by half, I stopped seeing Excel as a tool I had to use and started seeing it as a glimpse of something much bigger. If a few well-placed formulas could save days of work, what could a real programming language do? What could happen when scale stopped being a constraint, when I could test a hypothesis across millions of rows instead of a few thousand?

That question pulled me toward Python and SQL. I started small, cleaning datasets, writing queries, and automating tasks I'd once done by hand, but the more I built, the more I realised the real value wasn't in the code. It was in what the code revealed. A well-structured query could surface a story buried under thousands of transactions. A clean visualisation could turn a contested opinion into a decision everyone in the room could agree on. I noticed how financial arguments backed by sharp analytics and compelling visuals carried a different kind of weight. They didn't just inform decisions; they accelerated them.

That realisation became a turning point, but not the kind I expected. I didn't want to walk away from finance. I wanted to lean further into the part of it I loved most: the research, the modelling, the analytical rigour behind every recommendation. The operations side of investment banking had taught me how decisions get executed; now I wanted to spend my time on why they get made, and how data could make those answers sharper.

So I made a deliberate move: I came to the UK to pursue an MSc in Business Data Analytics at Bangor University. The decision was equal parts intimidating and clarifying. I wasn't trading finance for tech; I was equipping myself to do the more research-driven, analytical side of finance properly.

These days, my work lives at that intersection. Through modules like Financial Econometrics, Credit Risk Analytics, and FinTech, I'm building machine learning models, running time series analyses, and tackling problems that sit squarely in the financial analytics space: credit default probability, retail business analytics, customer churn, and beyond. Each project pushes me further from the analyst who once felt clever for nesting an IF statement, and closer to someone who can take a complex financial question and turn it into a defensible, data-driven answer.

What I've come to appreciate most is that my finance background isn't something I left behind; it's the thing that makes the analytics useful. Models matter, but so does knowing which question is worth asking, which variable actually drives risk, and which insight will hold up in front of a credit committee. A finance lens helps me see why a number matters; an analytics lens helps me prove it.

I'm still early in this chapter, and that's exactly the part I enjoy. Every project teaches me something new about data, about financial decision-making, and about the craft of turning numbers into narratives that people can act on.

If there's a thread running through all of it, it's this: the best financial decisions happen when business intuition and data rigour stop competing and start collaborating. That's the space I'm building my career in, and this blog is where I'll share what I learn along the way.

Key Idea

Tools become languages.

Excel was the gateway. Once it stopped being something to use and became something to think in, the question of what Python could do became unavoidable.

Key Idea

Finance and analytics are stronger together.

A finance lens shows why a number matters. An analytics lens proves it. The background isn't a detour; it's what makes the models meaningful.
